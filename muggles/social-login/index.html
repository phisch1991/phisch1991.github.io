Social Login

What happens if I click on Login with Facebook?

Before we start

Have you ever used - or at least seen - the button Login with Facebook on a new web application you wanted to use? I guess for most non-IT people it is quite mysterious what happens if you click this button: You are suddenly logged in, the website knows your name and e-mail address and you don’t even have to set another password for the new website.

We could now take a look at technical terms and specifications, but let’s start with a real world example everybody knows.

Step back for a second

How do you prove that you are allowed to drive cars when the police stops you? You take your drivers license and show it to the police officer. He/she then checks if the document is valid (i. e. not expired, not manipulated, etc.) Furthermore, the officer checks if the allowed vehicle classes on the license include the vehicle you currently drive.

Where did you get your drivers license from? I guess your drivers license was issued by a federal agency (“Bundesdruckerei” in Germany) after you proved that you are you (e. g. with your national ID) and that you passed the driving exam. Of course, you trust this federal agency, otherwise you would not have given them so many information about yourself.

Why does the police officer trust that you are allowed to drive cars? Obviously not (only) because you look trustworthy, but he trusts the federal agency which issued the license. Fortunately, this process is standardized, so at least within the EU other country’s police officers will trust your drivers license as well.

Social login

The term social login just means that you do not create another username/password combination for a new web application you use, but you use a social network where you probably have an account anyway to log into this new application.

What happens if you click on such a social login button? First of all, you will be redirected to the social network, where you either have to enter your username and password or are already logged in as you recently used it. Then you probably will be asked, if you agree, that the social network hands over some of your personal information (such as e-mail, given name, last name, etc.) to the new web application. Afterwards you will be redirected to the web application again and can use it. Although you did not actively create an account there, they know who you are.

How does that work? Let’s remember the police example from above.Â A long time ago, the federal agency checked that you are you. This process is called authentication. Afterwards they sent you a document called drivers license to you. This is called issuance. The same is happening with your social network. You created an account in your favorite social network (probably some time ago) and trusted them, that they will keep your information safe (well, many do not trust social networks, but share many information anyway, but that’s another point). The account (username and password) is necessary to authenticate yourself, just like the federal agency did. Afterwards they issued a document called token with some information about you. With this token you went to the new web application and show it. Now the new application knows the things “written” on this token and thus knows your identity.

Why does the new application know, that the document is valid? Well, the police proves the validity of your drivers license by visually inspecting it for manipulations, by checking the expiry dates on the document and by checking the vehicle classes. Similarly, the web application can check if you or somebody else manipulated the token (of course not by visual checks, but with kind of a digital signature) and any other information contained in the token. The signature even has another function: The web application can decide if it trusts the signer or not. Furthermore, the token holds information about the validity period, so old tokens can be declined.

Let’s get a bit more technical

The standard used for this kind of login is called OAuth2/OIDCI. I do not want to bother you with the details of the specification, however I would like to sum up the whole story and link it to some technial terms. I guess you already noticed, that some of the words in the text are italic. We will now compare them in the table below:

Police Example Social Login Example What’s the correct term for it?
Drivers license Token Token
Police officer Web application Resource server and client
You You Resource Owner

I hope you now have at least a little understanding how things work :-)

What I didn’t mention

Of course, there are some slight differences between the police example and social login:

  • While the validity period of your drivers license is several years, tokens usually expire very quickly. That is why your social network needs to issue tokens more frequently than your federal agency needs to issue drivers licenses.
  • The communication process above is very simplified and of course more complex in reality. However, the overall principle is the same: You trust the social network, the web application trusts the social network, so the web application also kind of trusts you, although there is no direct relationship between the web application and you.
